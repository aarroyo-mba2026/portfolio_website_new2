Portfolio website design brief

Design a personal portfolio website in English for a Product Manager based in London, open to opportunities across London and Europe. The website should feel strategic, polished, modern, and highly intentional. It should position me as a product leader with strong design sensibility, systems thinking, and execution depth, not as a visual designer or freelancer. My current site already communicates product management, experimentation, analytics, case studies, a product thinking lab, and openness to PM roles, so keep those foundations but elevate them significantly in clarity, credibility, and visual quality.  ￼

Core positioning

The site should communicate this profile:
	•	Product Manager
	•	Strong in 0 to 1 product work
	•	Strong in post-launch iteration
	•	Quantitative and analytical
	•	Strong at cross-functional execution
	•	Thoughtful about product thinking, not just shipping features
	•	Based in London, open across Europe

Overall creative direction

Blend these three reference qualities into one coherent product-focused portfolio:
	1.	Project-first, visual-first structure
Prioritise selected work high on the homepage, make location visible near the top, place About lower down, and end with a very clear contact section. The Jorge reference does this well with a strong “Selected Works” area near the top, clear city mention, About later, and a clean “Get in touch” ending.  ￼
	2.	Personal but sharp hero section
The hero should feel human and direct, with a short introduction, clear title, and clear call to action. The Kane Sherwell reference is useful here because it centres the person, uses a compact intro, and keeps the CTA immediate and visible.  ￼
	3.	High-polish interactions and premium footer/contact area
The site should feel refined in hover behaviour, transitions, and final contact experience. The Lauren Thacker reference is useful for its “Hover to explore” interaction language and especially for its final contact section with “READY WHEN YOU ARE” and multiple reasons to reach out.  ￼

Tone and voice

The writing should sound:
	•	concise
	•	sharp
	•	confident
	•	intelligent
	•	human
	•	grounded
	•	product-led
	•	not fluffy
	•	not overly corporate
	•	not overly playful
	•	not “creative agency” style
	•	not generic tech-bro language

Avoid:
	•	“I create magic”
	•	“passionate about innovation”
	•	“digital experiences that delight” style clichés
	•	overly long paragraphs
	•	buzzword-heavy product copy

The tone should feel like someone who can work with executives, engineers, designers, and business stakeholders, and who can take ambiguous problems into clear product decisions.

Visual design direction

The website should feel:
	•	premium
	•	editorial
	•	modern
	•	calm
	•	structured
	•	high taste
	•	very legible

Use:
	•	large project thumbnails or mockup-led project cards
	•	strong spacing and clean composition
	•	restrained but sophisticated typography
	•	subtle contrast and hierarchy
	•	a light background or soft warm neutral background
	•	a refined accent colour with strong contrast
	•	avoid a heavy all-black aesthetic

Colour direction:
	•	off-white / cream / light neutral base
	•	deep charcoal or near-black text
	•	one elegant accent colour only
	•	the palette should feel premium and calm, not trendy for the sake of trend

Typography direction:
	•	bold, confident headlines
	•	compact and readable body text
	•	visible hierarchy between headline, kicker, body, labels, and metadata
	•	slightly editorial feel, but still highly readable for product hiring managers

Animation and interaction specs

Motion should feel expensive and controlled, not flashy.

Use:
	•	smooth fade/slide transitions between sections
	•	subtle reveal on scroll
	•	soft hover responses on cards, links, and buttons
	•	high-quality easing
	•	transitions that feel natural and deliberate
	•	premium micro-interactions on project cards and CTA buttons

Interaction principles:
	•	no gimmicky animation
	•	no excessive parallax
	•	no distracting auto-motion
	•	all motion should support clarity and polish

Specific behaviour:
	•	project cards should slightly respond on hover
	•	capability/value cards should highlight on hover
	•	links should have subtle directionality or underline treatment
	•	buttons should have crisp feedback
	•	the site should feel smooth when scrolling between sections
	•	the final contact section should feel especially polished and intentional

Borrow inspiration from:
	•	the natural flow and visual emphasis of Jorge’s project-first homepage structure  ￼
	•	the direct hero and CTA clarity of Kane Sherwell’s homepage  ￼
	•	the polished interactive feel and footer framing on Lauren Thacker’s site, including “Hover to explore” and the “READY WHEN YOU ARE” contact language pattern  ￼

Information architecture

The homepage should follow this order:

1. Header / navigation

Simple top nav with:
	•	About
	•	Work
	•	Product Lab
	•	Contact

Optional sticky navigation, but keep it subtle.

2. Hero section

The hero should be compact, sharp, and immediately clear.

Include:
	•	Name
	•	Role: Product Manager
	•	Short positioning statement
	•	Location line: Based in London, open to London and Europe
	•	Primary CTA: View selected work
	•	Secondary CTA: Let’s talk or Get in touch

Hero copy should not be long. It should feel more like a strong opening than a bio paragraph.

The hero should communicate:
	•	I build and shape digital products
	•	I work from strategy through execution
	•	I care about measurable outcomes
	•	I’m available for PM opportunities

3. Selected work

This section should appear very high on the page. Project work should be the main focus, as in the Jorge reference where “Selected Works” is prioritised early.  ￼

Use large visual cards with:
	•	project title
	•	one-line impact or outcome statement
	•	category tags
	•	your role
	•	optional company or industry
	•	strong image or mockup
	•	hover state
	•	click-through to case study

Case study previews should feel like:
	•	product-led
	•	strategic
	•	credible
	•	visual enough to feel polished
	•	not overly decorative

4. Product value proposition / capabilities section

This is the equivalent of the “What do I help?” section in Kane’s site, but rewritten for a Product Manager, not for a web freelancer.  ￼

Possible section titles:
	•	What I bring
	•	How I work
	•	Where I create value
	•	What I help build

Content should communicate capabilities such as:
	•	product strategy
	•	product definition
	•	prioritisation
	•	roadmap shaping
	•	experimentation
	•	analytics and measurement
	•	cross-functional execution
	•	stakeholder alignment
	•	launching and iterating products

This section can be shown as:
	•	hover cards
	•	short capability tiles
	•	structured blocks
	•	elegant two-column layout

5. Brands / companies / trust section

Include a trust section with company names or logos. This is inspired by Kane Sherwell’s use of brand credibility signals.  ￼

Goal:
	•	show credibility fast
	•	reassure hiring managers
	•	make experience feel real and grounded

6. About section

Place About below work and value proposition, not too high on the homepage. That structure is closer to the Jorge reference and works well.  ￼

The About section should be short and strategic, not autobiographical.

It should cover:
	•	who I am professionally
	•	how I approach product work
	•	what kind of teams and products I thrive in
	•	that I am based in London and open to Europe

7. Product Lab section

Keep a section for Product Lab because it already exists on my current site and is a useful differentiator. It should feel more premium and integrated than it does now. My current site describes it as feature ideas and critiques for products I admire, designed to be shared externally. Preserve that intent.  ￼

This section should present:
	•	feature ideas
	•	product critiques
	•	lightweight speculative PM thinking
	•	concept explorations

It should not feel like a blog dump. It should feel curated, intelligent, and selective.

Use:
	•	editorial cards
	•	short title + 1 sentence
	•	category tag
	•	subtle hover behaviour

8. Final contact section

The page must end with a strong, memorable contact section.

Use this structure:
	•	small intro line: Let’s work together
	•	larger main line: Ready when you are
	•	one sentence making it easy to contact me for hiring, product roles, collaboration, or a product conversation

This pattern is directly inspired by the Lauren Thacker footer, which frames outreach clearly and makes the contact area feel intentional rather than generic.  ￼

Include:
	•	Email
	•	LinkedIn
	•	CV / Resume
	•	Contact form

The form should feel simple and high-end, not clunky.

The contact framing can include:
	•	Hiring for a Product Manager role
	•	Interested in collaboration
	•	Want to talk product
	•	Want to discuss strategy or product thinking

Case study expectations

Even if the homepage is visual, the project detail pages should be clearly PM-oriented.

Each case study should show:
	•	context
	•	problem
	•	product opportunity
	•	your role
	•	constraints or trade-offs
	•	how decisions were made
	•	how you worked cross-functionally
	•	what changed
	•	result or outcome
	•	visuals that support the story

Avoid making the case studies feel like design portfolios full of mockups with little product reasoning.

What to preserve from my current website

Preserve these ideas from my current site, but redesign them with much higher quality:
	•	clear Product Management positioning
	•	emphasis on 0 to 1 and post-launch iteration
	•	quantitative edge in experimentation and analytics
	•	selected case studies
	•	Product Lab / feature idea section
	•	direct contact CTA for Product Management roles  ￼

What to improve from my current website

Improve:
	•	stronger hierarchy
	•	stronger visual impact
	•	more premium spacing and typography
	•	clearer product positioning
	•	more intentional transitions
	•	better distinction between sections
	•	stronger project-first impression
	•	more refined contact/footer area
	•	more polished interaction quality

Final quality bar

This should feel like the portfolio of a serious, modern Product Manager who is:
	•	strategic
	•	credible
	•	visually sharp
	•	thoughtful
	•	execution-oriented
	•	easy to hire
	•	differentiated by taste and product thinking

Do not make it feel like:
	•	a generic template
	•	a designer portfolio pretending to be product
	•	a corporate resume site
	•	a startup landing page
	•	a noisy creative portfolio

Make it feel like a carefully designed, product-led personal site that could impress:
	•	hiring managers
	•	heads of product
	•	founders
	•	design-conscious tech teams
